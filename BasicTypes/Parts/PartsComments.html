Word like

  • Token. Hopefully a useful abstraction for all the below types of parts
  • Word. Fixed set of words. Can be glossed to a foreign language. Usually a content word.
  • Numbers. Can generate more of them. Behave somewhat like compound words. Can gloss to a somewhat universal format of Arabic numbers algorithmicly without a dictionary. Explicit starts with # and has dashes.
  • Compound Word. Essentially any noun or verb phrase (or sometimes sentence) that can be used as head noun, verb. Modifiers can be infixed. Explicit has dashes.
  • Proper Modifier. Must be capitalized. Can't be a head of a headed phrase. Can be glossed from dictionary.
  • Foreign Word. Can't be glossed. Syntactically like Compound Words. Can't infix modifiers. Explicit wrapped in ""
  • Neologism. Phonotactically valid word, but it is not a word. Can't be glossed. Content words only. Explicit starts with +.
  • Onomatopoeia. Special type of proper modifier. Details not worked out.
  • Tags. Reduplication, question phrases, conjunctions, negations. Sort of a murky concept. The key is they (can) have limited scope, as limited as 1 word. So at the moment, I assume they always have scope of 1 word.
  • Particle. Different from the rest of the content words. More constrained by rules than content words. In the case of prepositions, these overlap with content words.

Token like, but not word like

  • Punctuation. Particularly end of sentence punctuation-- !, ? and : add meaning that is relevantly different from . and not available in the sentence
  • Template. Least token-like concept. {0} li pona tawa {1} ==> {1} likes {0}

Tokens. The most generic, atomic piece of syntax.
